# Bouillon vs Stock

Working notebook. When to reach for bouillon cubes (or better-than-bouillon paste) vs liquid stock (the kind that goes bad in the fridge).

## Bouillon cubes / better-than-bouillon

Concentrated, salty, and filled with MSG. More flavorful per unit than liquid stock — that's both the upside and the limit. Right call in three situations:

- **Soups where the broth is eaten with a spoon.** The broth IS the dish, so you want it loud and savory. Diluted stock would read weak; bouillon delivers the flavor density a broth-forward soup wants.
- **Comfort food / gravy territory.** Gravies, pot pies, mushroom-heavy braises, anything where the goal is rich and meaty-tasting. The MSG and concentrated meat flavor are doing the work the dish is asking for.
- **Cooking meat in it (especially mexican dishes).** When meat simmers in bouillon liquid, the cube's collagen and meat-flavor concentrates layer onto the meat's own rendering — you get a richer, more meat-tasting result than starting with plain stock. Particularly useful in mexican meat-and-bean stews (chile colorado is a notable exception — see below).

## Liquid stock

The refrigerated, perishable kind. Gentler body, less concentrated, no MSG. Right for everything that's NOT broth-as-the-dish or comfort-food-rich:

- **Most sauces.** Pan sauces, reductions, braises where the stock is being reduced and concentrated as part of the cooking — bouillon would over-concentrate to harsh.
- **Bean dishes.** Including refried beans, bean stews, lentil soups. Beans want a more layered, less aggressive liquid; bouillon makes bean dishes read salty and one-note. This is the chile colorado lesson and the amy's lentil soup lesson.
- **Subtle vegetable-led soups** where the stock is supporting, not headlining.

## The ambiguous middle

**Mole and mexican sauces that call for stock** can go either way. The dish has enough other concentrated components (chiles, spices, nuts/seeds, chocolate in some) that the bouillon-vs-stock choice doesn't dominate the result. Pick based on what else is in the pot — if the dish is meat-forward, bouillon. If it's chile-and-aromatic forward, stock.

## Rule of thumb

If the broth is what you're tasting → bouillon.
If the broth is what's carrying everything else → stock.
