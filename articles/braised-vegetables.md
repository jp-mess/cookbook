# Braised Vegetables

Working notebook. Vegetable by vegetable: braising worth it or not?

Braising = small amount of liquid (with fat, sometimes sugar/aromatics), covered pan, until liquid reduces and coats the vegetable. Cooks the vegetable AND concentrates a sauce in one move.

## Carrots — yes

The strongest case in the cabinet.

- Faster than steaming or baking. Liquid contact + trapped steam at once; most other methods only give one.
- Butter-and-sugar glazed carrots aren't reproducible by any other technique. Half vegetable, half candy.
- Three-knob system (water, butter, sugar). Optional adds: tarragon (canonical), thyme, citrus, aromatics. Predictable adjustments.

No reason to cook carrots any other way as a side.

## Turnips and Radishes — no

Both fail braising for the same reason.

- Get funky under heat — sulfurous/bitter character that's faint when raw amplifies under sauté or braise.
- Working alternatives: raw and thin in salads, or fully submerged in salty broth-based stews (borscht, beef stew) where salt and volume dilute the funk.
- Braising is the wrong middle ground — too much heat to stay clean, not enough liquid to neutralize.

## Endives — no

- Heat destroys both qualities that make endives worth eating: crunchiness (collapses) and nuttiness (bitter sulfurous bottom notes amplify, nutty top notes mute).
- French braised-endives-in-cream tradition exists; the dairy is masking the failure. Mask = wrong technique.

Endives are a salad green.

## Celery — yes (peel first)

- Peel hard outer fibers off each stalk before cooking. Without this: soft inside, stringy outside. Make-or-break step. Vegetable peeler down the convex side of each stalk, ~1 min/stalk.
- Once peeled, braising softens celery to silky in a way raw (too crunchy) or sautéed (squeaky) can't.
- Canonical: marcella hazan's lemon-and-potato celery braise (`marcella-lemon-celery`). Celery + potatoes + olive oil + salt + lemon, shallow water, until water evaporates.
- Also works in chicken-and-vegetable soups, pot pie filling, fish-dish bases.

## Potatoes — yes, with opportunity cost

Braised potatoes work. But potatoes have better things to do with water and heat:

- Parboiled → home fries / roasted. Crisp exterior + soft interior. Braising can't produce this.
- Boiled → mashed. Braising leaves potatoes structurally intact, opposite of what mash wants.

Yes when potatoes are riding alongside another braise (the celery dish, chicken-and-vegetable pans). No when they're the headliner.

## Onions — yes, only the vinegar braise

Narrow yes: braising in vinegar to make a quick faux pickle.

- Canonical: red onions sliced thin, briefly braised in apple cider vinegar with olive oil and lemon zest (`faux-pickled-red-onions`).
- Red onions specifically — color holds up under vinegar (white goes grey, yellow looks unappetizing); flavor suits salads.
- Beats raw red onions (too sharp; vinaigrette alone won't mellow them in normal eating time) and sautéed (loses the structural crunch a salad onion needs).

Outside vinegar pickling, onions don't benefit from braising as a side.

## Leeks — no

Not for funkiness — braised leeks are edible. The reason is opportunity cost.

- Better technique for leeks-as-headliner: cut into 2–3 inch dense cylinders, stand on end, olive oil + salt, bake until caramelized outside / silky inside. Concentrates the sugars; cylinder format keeps layers from going gummy.
- Braised-leeks-vinaigrette = same problem as braised endives. Vinaigrette masks the texture loss.
- Leeks-as-aromatic in stocks and bases (french grandma soup, fish poaches, mushroom-leek galettes) is a different question — gumminess doesn't matter when leeks are dispersed.
