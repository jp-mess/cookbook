# Nuts and Seeds

Working notebook. Observations on individual nuts and seeds, organized by ingredient.

## Pine Nuts

Pine nuts have an asymmetric relationship with pesto:

- They're the **optimal nut for pesto** — no other nut sits as cleanly into a basil-and-cheese green sauce.
- But **pesto is not their optimal use**. Pesto can be made with walnuts, almonds, or no nuts at all (a shaved-parmesan pesto or a basil-only sauce work). The pine-nut flavor is mostly absorbed and flattened into the green base.

Where pine nuts actually shine is anywhere they get to keep their identity as a discrete, browned, fatty seed:

- Sprinkled on flatbreads and pizzas (especially green-sauce pizzas, where the toasted pine nuts are the clearest texture and flavor contrast against the soft sauce).
- In savory tarts — swiss chard, spinach, leek-and-cheese.
- In pastas with raisins (the canonical italian sweet-savory pairing — sicilian-style chard-and-raisin pasta, sardine-pasta variants).

The economic argument: pine nuts are expensive. Burying them in a blender erases the thing that justifies the cost. Toasting them whole on top of something — where each nut keeps its color, crunch, and aroma — is where you actually taste them.

You can make pesto without pine nuts. Try finding a better nut for the top of a green-sauce pizza.

## Sunflower Seeds

The opposite asymmetry from pine nuts: sunflower seeds have one undisputed best use AND a useful supporting role almost everywhere else.

- **Optimal use: on toast / tartines / bagels with cream cheese.** Nothing else competes here. Sesame is the obvious challenger and isn't close.
- **Pesto extender.** Sunflower isn't the optimal nut for any single pesto, but it cuts any other nut cleanly. The two pestos I keep using:
  - 50/50 sunflower + pine nut in basil pesto.
  - 50/50 sunflower + almond in red pesto.
  - **Sunflower + cashew** is a particularly rewarding combination — close in flavor to straight pine nuts, at a fraction of the cost.
- **Salads.** Decent protein addition, but generally outclassed by slivered almonds — almonds bring more crunch and a more interesting toasted flavor.

## Almonds

The most versatile nut — almonds slot into more roles than any other entry in this article.

- **Slivered almonds: best nut for salads.** More crunch, more toasted depth, and visually distinctive in a way other nuts aren't. Apple-fennel salad, root-vegetable salad, anywhere a salad wants a protein crunch.
- **Best baking nut for cakes with fruity overtones** — lemon cake, strawberry cake, anything in the citrus-and-stone-fruit dessert range. Almond's slight marzipan character pairs naturally with fruit acids in a way walnut or pecan doesn't.
- **Backbone of red tomato pestos and romesco.** Where pine nuts anchor green pesto, almonds anchor red. The combination of almond + sun-dried tomato (red pesto) or almond + roasted red pepper + tomato (romesco) is a structural italian/spanish pairing. The reason almonds slot into red pestos better than green isn't just flavor — it's processing. Almonds need a blender or food processor to puree; a mortar and pestle isn't strong enough. Green pestos are best made with a mortar (basil bruises in a blender, and a mortar produces a less aerated, more flavorful sauce). Red pestos and romesco want a blender anyway — peppers can't be smashed with a pestle, and tomatoes carry enough liquid to make a smooth blend without forcing in too much oil or cream. So tool and ingredient line up: green pesto + mortar wants pine nuts; red pesto + blender wants almonds. In theory slivered almonds and sun-dried tomatoes could be mortared together, but slivered almonds have better things to do (salads and cake topping).
- **Whole, in indian curries.** Less common in this cookbook than the other roles, but real — chana madra is the canonical example, where whole almonds are simmered into the curry alongside raisins. The almond/raisin combination shows up across indian, sicilian, and middle-eastern cooking and isn't well replicated by other nuts.

## Cashews

Niche uses; high-fat content is the unifying property.

- **Blended into indian dishes** — korma especially. Cashews puréed into the sauce give it the rich, slightly sweet, dairy-adjacent body that defines that style. Other nuts don't substitute well; cashew's combination of softness, fat, and neutral flavor is hard to replicate.
- **Green pesto base.** Surprisingly competitive substitute for pine nuts in basil pesto — the high fat content gives the sauce the same emulsified, glossy texture pine nuts provide. Sunflower + cashew (covered in the sunflower seeds section) is particularly close to straight pine nut.

Outside these two slots cashews show up less often. Their flavor is too neutral to be a structural ingredient on its own; they're best in roles where they're carrying texture and fat rather than a distinct nut character.

## Pumpkin Seeds

Three strong slots, plus a weaker version of the sunflower-seed role.

- **Optimal for mexican cooking.** Pumpkin seeds (pepitas) are central to mole verde, salsas, and other green-sauce mexican dishes — toasted and blended into the sauce as a structural thickener and earthy nut note. No other seed sits as cleanly into the mexican green-sauce family. This is pumpkin seeds' clearest niche.
- **Universal pesto extender** (like sunflower). Cuts other nuts cleanly without taking over the flavor. Useful when you want to stretch a pesto or temper a strong-flavored nut.
- **Best seed for dessert and cake toppings.** Pumpkin seeds are the right choice on top of cakes, quick breads, and other desserts — they hold their shape under heat, have an attractive dark-green color against frosting or crumb, and their slightly bitter-earthy flavor balances sweet bases better than sunflower or sesame do. Sunflower seeds work too but read flatter on dessert; pumpkin seeds bring more presence.
- **Tartines and bagels** — work, but outclassed by sunflower seeds for this role. The texture is similar but the flavor is more vegetal and less rich; sunflower wins the cream-cheese pairing.

## Sesame Seeds

Similar role profile to pumpkin seeds — mexican blender sauces and bread toppings — with one important handling caveat.

- **Mexican blender sauces.** Toasted and blended into mole and salsas alongside (or instead of) pumpkin seeds. The two seeds are roughly substitutable in this slot, though sesame brings a slightly nuttier, oilier character.
- **Bread toppings — but only baked-in, not sprinkled-on.** Sesame seeds are good bread toppings, but the loose, small format makes them too messy for sprinkling onto avocado toast or bagels with cream cheese — they roll off, scatter, and end up everywhere except in your mouth. The right move is to embed them into the bread itself during baking, where the sticky dough surface holds them down. After-the-fact sprinkling is sunflower territory; sesame wants a controlled bake.

## Walnuts

The best universal baking nut.

- **Cakes and cookies, broadly.** Walnuts fit virtually every cake and cookie — banana bread, brownies, oatmeal cookies, carrot cake, coffee cake, yogurt cake. Almonds work too in some of these, but walnuts have a more flexible flavor that doesn't push the dessert toward marzipan-and-fruit territory the way almonds do.
- **Easier to prepare than almonds.** Walnuts crush, crumble, and toast easily. Almonds are hardy enough that prepping a platter of toasted slivered almonds — say, for the top of a lemon cake — is a real chore. Walnuts give you the same toasted-nut role with much less effort.
- **Salads, but second to almonds.** Walnuts work in salads, especially toasted, but slivered almonds beat them for crunch and visual cleanness. Untoasted walnuts in salad are noticeably worse — the raw flavor is slightly tannic and doesn't integrate the way a toasted nut does.

The split: walnuts win baking, almonds win salads.
