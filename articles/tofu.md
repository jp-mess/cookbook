# Tofu

Working notebook. Four types of tofu show up in grocery stores: silken, firm, extra/super firm, and shelf-stable. Each one has its own water content, texture, and best-use slot. Sections below are stubs for now.

## Silken Tofu

Refrigerated silken tofu is too soft for the use most packages recommend (cubed into soups and broths). It falls apart, dilutes the broth, and the texture is closer to wet curd than to a structural ingredient. For broth applications, **shelf-stable tofu** is the right call instead (covered below).

Where refrigerated silken actually belongs is as a base for derived sauces, dressings, and baking applications — anywhere its softness becomes an asset rather than a problem:

- **Pudding.** Blended silken tofu makes the body of a good chocolate or coffee pudding.
- **Fruit salad dressing.** Pureed with citrus, honey, or vanilla for a creamy fruit-salad sauce.
- **Yogurt replacement in quickbreads** — reported to work but I haven't tested it yet. Want-to-try.

**Handling notes:**
- No pressing required. The water content is the texture; squeezing it out defeats the point.
- A double boiler can be used to firm it up (proteins set the same way they do in eggs). Useful when you want a slightly more structured pudding or dressing — gives you a knob between liquid-blended and firmly-set.

## Firm Tofu

The most flexible of the four types. There's a well-known decision tree, with three axes: prep method, cut shape, and optional starch coating.

**Step 1 — remove the aquafaba.** Before cutting or tearing, firm tofu needs the funky packaging liquid driven off. Two methods, with different downstream consequences:

- **Press** (tofu press, ~few hours): leaves the tofu denser and drier. Best for tofu you'll bake in the oven afterwards — the dryness lets the surface caramelize without weeping water.
- **Boil**: loosens the tofu and makes it better at absorbing spiced oil. Best when you're going to sear it in a pan.

You don't really pick one over the other in the abstract — you pick based on what cooking method comes next.

**Step 2 — pick a cut.** Three options, each suited to a different final dish:

- **Tear into chunks** (only works if you pressed, not boiled — boiled tofu falls apart). Reads like torn meat. Optimal for tikka masala and other curries with thick sauces, where the irregular surface holds onto the sauce the way braised meat would.
- **Cube.** Maximizes surface area per piece and makes the tofu easy to toss around with other ingredients. The canonical stir-fry cut.
- **Thin wide slices.** Turns the tofu into a vehicle for a breaded coating or a hash-brown-style pan crisp. Use when the tofu is the central plate component (not buried in noodles or vegetables) — the slice is what gets seared and seasoned.

**Step 3 — optional starch coating.** Corn or potato starch tossed onto the tofu before cooking gives it a crisp shell. This works best on **cubed tofu** specifically — cubes have enough surface area for the starch to do useful work AND they're easy to toss to coat evenly. Slices and chunks work less cleanly with starch (slices want breadcrumbs instead; chunks want to absorb sauce, not repel it).

## Extra / Super Firm Tofu

Generally inferior to home-pressed firm tofu for almost everything. In theory extra/super firm is just pre-pressed firm tofu — but doing the press yourself (the extra labor) produces a better result. The factory-pressed version comes out denser-feeling but blander, with a slightly compressed texture that reads less like tofu and more like a protein brick.

The one slot where extra/super firm earns its place: **true crumbles**. For tacos and other dishes that want tofu pretending to be ground meat, you need the tofu to break into small, dry, structural crumbles rather than wet curd. Home-pressed firm tofu doesn't get firm enough for this extreme — it'll still hold together a bit or release moisture when broken up. Extra/super firm is dry and dense enough to crumble cleanly.

So: home-press firm tofu by default; reach for extra/super firm only when the dish specifically needs the crumble texture.

## Shelf-Stable Tofu

Basically silken tofu set in aspic — same soft interior, but the shelf-stable packaging gives it a firmer outer structure that holds its shape when cut. This is what the silken section's "for broth applications, reach for shelf-stable instead" was pointing at.

**Best use:** chopped into cubes and dropped into sauces (curries) or soups as extra protein. The cubes hold together where refrigerated silken would dissolve, and the soft interior absorbs the surrounding sauce without going gummy.

**No prep, no processing.** Don't press it, don't bake it, don't sear it, don't blend it. It tastes good as-is and the texture is the point — cooking or processing it ruins what makes it work. It's a finishing-protein move: open the package, cube, drop into the hot pot, eat.
